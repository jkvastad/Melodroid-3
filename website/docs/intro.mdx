# Melodroid

A program for music research from first principles. 
Melodroid explores some physically motivated patterns of sound in relation to the biology and behavior of human hearing in hopes of providing a framework for composing melody and harmony.

Melodroid is mainly a CLI program producing various plots and printouts for study. This companion website works as docs, explaining the theory and providing some try-it-yourself experiments via browser synths.

## Melody and Harmony
What is melody and harmony? Intuitively we may say it is a singer and a band, or perhaps the first violin and the orchestra, or the soprano and the choir.

But what is going on behind the scenes? Why do some melodies sound better than others? Why do some sequences of chords sound better than others? 
We obviously like some sort of structured sound, but what is this structure? In the [related research chapter](/docs/category/related-research) I collect some notable research with interactive reproductions of experiments where possible. 

Melodroid will specifically look at what I call Harmonic Pattern Theory (HPT), so named because of the inspiration from pattern matching of certain frequency sets. 
In HPT the idea is to say that the brain with its trillions of synapses has a lot of potential complexity as to the why of things, but perhaps we are more fortunate in the physical world. 
By looking at the patterns which the brain has access to today and through its evolution, we can perhaps gain some fundamental insight into the structure of music.

## Harmonic Pattern Theory

We study musical harmony inspired by **pattern recognition of simultaneous waves**. For example, a major
chord can be expressed as fractions $\{1,\ 5/4,\ 3/2\}$. The pattern for this set of frequencies
loops every four iterations of the base wavelength, extending over four periods of time.

As can be seen, this creates a pattern of waves over time. The **wave pattern length** (WPL) is the least common multiple (LCM) of the set's denominators —
for a chord $\{p_1/q_1,\ \ldots,\ p_n/q_n\}$ the WPL is:

$$
\mathrm{WPL} = \operatorname{lcm}(q_1,\ q_2,\ \ldots,\ q_n).
$$

Since the human voice produces harmonic overtones, the brain might have evolved to find these kinds of patterns in sound and use them for our perception of music, especially for harmony.

### Wave Pattern Duration

Given the lower frequency threshold of human hearing at about $20\ \mathrm{Hz}$, a wave pattern lasting more than roughly $50\ \mathrm{ms}$ is unlikely to be recognised as a pattern.
For melody, the [lower limit of melodic pitch](related-research/wave-pattern-length.mdx#lower-limit-of-melodic-pitch) is closer to 30hz, allowing the wave pattern only some 33ms. 
This is merely a ball-park estimate on upper bounds of pattern complexity, as the brain might in practice have mechanisms to exploit patterns before they are fully formed.

The **wave pattern duration** (WPD) for a fundamental $f_0$ is

$$
\mathrm{WPD} = \mathrm{WPL} \cdot \frac{1000}{f_0}\ \mathrm{ms}.
$$

For a fundamental of $100\ \mathrm{Hz}$, the major chord (WPL $4$) gives
$\mathrm{WPD} = 4 \cdot \frac{1000}{100} = 40\ \mathrm{ms}$.

Music (at least orchestra and opera) lives mostly in the C2–C6 range (≈ 65–1047 Hz fundamental). Lower frequencies produce longer-duration patterns, so
$100\ \mathrm{Hz}$ serves as a useful worst case for WPD. 
We will show in the theory section how this simple constraint could lead to consequences for everything from chord progressions to an optimal number of keys for a piano.

Of note is that the pattern matching idea is a form of autocorrelation. 
Another idea entirely is [neural resonance](related-research/wave-pattern-length.mdx#neural-resonance), where instead of recognising a pattern, neural oscillators in the brain mode-lock to small integer ratios.
In such a case the neuronal stability of certain integer ratios is more important than duration as a wave pattern is effectively perceived in parallell.
