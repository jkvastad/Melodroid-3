# Consonance
When two or more frequencies sound simultaneously they are perceived as more or less pleasant. This is called consonance. The reasons for consonance have perhaps been studied for millenia - already the babylonians had tuning systems:  West, M. L. "The Babylonian Musical Notation and the Hurrian Melodic Texts." Music & Letters 75, no. 2 (1994): 161–179.

In modern times there are three primary hypotheses for consonance "Musical consonance: a review of theory and evidence on perception and preference of auditory roughness in humans and other animals"
* Aversion to roughness
* Preference for harmonicity
* Learned preference from cultural exposure

Aversion to roughness centers around Helmholtz observation (TODO: citation for Helmholtz original work) that as two coinciding frequencies are separated, there occurs a maxima of unpleasantness. 
This creates a "dissonance curve".


Preference for harmonicity centers around the idea that humans like it when interval and overtone ratios are small simple fractions e.g. the octaves 2:1 or the perfect fifths 3:2.


Learned preference centers around the idea that musical preference is a learned phenomena.

## Timbral Effects
The pleasantness of simultaneous chord is affected not only by frequency ratios, but also by timbre (overtone/partials profiles).
In ["Timbral effects on consonance disentangle psychoacoustic mechanisms and suggest perceptual origins for musical scales"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11258268/)[^marjieh],
the authors run a series of experiments showing that consonance judgements are affected by not just the intervals (harmonicity) but by overtone profiles as well (aversion to roughness). 
These experiments are reimplemented below for the reader to experience for themselves:

### Stretched overtones and intervals
One observation is that the consonance of the ubiquitous octave is dependent on timbre. 
In the below graph, try adjusting both the timbre stretch from 1.9 to 2.1, and the interval from major seventh (11 semitones) to minor ninth (13 semitones). 
Press play, then drag the sliders or press "match" to snap the interval onto the timbre's spectrum lock.

Both notes share the same overtone profile: with harmonic partials ($\gamma = 2.0$) the plain octave (12 semitones) locks into consonance. 
As the partials are stretched or compressed the consonant interval drifts away from 12 semitones — the octave is consonant only relative to its timbre.

The red dashed line marks $12\log_2\gamma$ above the lower note - the semitone where all partials lock. 
This is where the upper note's frequency ratio equals the stretch $\gamma$, so every upper partial lands on a lower partial and the whole spectrum locks.

### Mixed timbres with a bonang 
From Study 2C we can listen to a dyad with **two different timbres**: the lower note is an ordinary harmonic tone with four equally-weighted partials (1, 2, 3, 4), while the higher note is modelled on a Javanese gamelan instrument called a bonang.
The bonang in the study uses overtones 𝑓0,1.52⁢𝑓0,3.46⁢𝑓0, and 3.92⁢𝑓0. 
In the graph below you can adjust semitone distance (0-15) for the higher note. Slendro scale steps are marked and partials are displayed.

<TimbreMorphPlayer

  rolloffDbPerOct={0}

  initial={4.8}
  scaleMarks={[2.4, 4.8, 7.2, 9.6, 12, 14.4]}
  label="▶ bonang dyad"
/>

Both tones use **equally-weighted** partials. Because the two spectra differ there is no single stretch factor, and so no partial-lock line — instead consonance appears wherever a bonang partial happens to fall on a harmonic of the lower note. Drag the upper voice and watch the sweep map: the lower tone's partials stay at fixed multiples while the bonang's slide upward, and the readout flags the closest cross-note pair. The study finds consonance peaks at **≈ 2.6** and **≈ 4.8 semitones** — exactly where the bonang's $3.46\,f_0$ and $1.52\,f_0$ partials meet the lower tone's 4th and 2nd harmonics (you can see the tracks cross). These sit on the marked *slendro* degrees (an equal five-tone division of the octave, ~240 cents per step), which the authors read as a perceptual origin for the scale.
