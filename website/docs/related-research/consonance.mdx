# Consonance
When two or more frequencies sound simultaneously they are perceived as more or less pleasant. This is called consonance. The reasons for consonance have perhaps been studied for millenia - already the babylonians had tuning systems:  West, M. L. "The Babylonian Musical Notation and the Hurrian Melodic Texts." Music & Letters 75, no. 2 (1994): 161–179.

In modern times there are three primary hypotheses for consonance "Musical consonance: a review of theory and evidence on perception and preference of auditory roughness in humans and other animals"
* Aversion to roughness
* Preference for harmonicity
* Learned preference from cultural exposure

Aversion to roughness centers around Helmholtz observation that as two coinciding frequencies are separated, there occurs a maxima of unpleasantness. This creates a dissonance curve.
Preference for harmonicity centers around the idea that humans like it when interval and overtone ratios are small simple fractions e.g. the octaves 2:1 or the perfect fifths 3:2.
Learned preference centers around the idea that musical preference is a learned phenomena.

## Timbral Effects
The pleasantness of simultaneous chord is affected not only by frequency ratios, but by timbre (overtone profiles). In ["Timbral effects on consonance disentangle psychoacoustic mechanisms and suggest perceptual origins for musical scales"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11258268/)[^marjieh]
the authors run a series of experiments showing that consonance judgements are affected by not just the intervals (harmonicity) but by overtone profiles as well (aversion to roughness). 
These experiments are reimplemented below for the reader to experience for themselves:

### Stretched overtones and intervals
One observation is that the consonance of the ubiquitous octave is dependent on the timbre. 
In the below graph, try adjusting both the timbre stretch from 1.9 to 2.1, and the interval from major seventh (11 semitones) to minor ninth (13 semitones). Press play, then drag the sliders. Both notes share the same overtone profile: with harmonic partials ($\gamma = 2.0$) the plain octave (12 semitones) locks into consonance, but as the partials are stretched or compressed the consonant interval drifts off 12 — the octave is consonant only relative to its timbre. The map plots each partial's frequency against the interval; where a track of the upper note crosses one of the lower note, the partials coincide and the tone settles. The red dashed line marks the **partial-lock interval** $12\log_2\gamma$ (measured above the lower note): the unique interval where the upper note's frequency ratio equals the stretch $\gamma$, so *every* upper partial lands on a lower partial at once and the whole spectrum locks. At $\gamma = 2.0$ it sits on the plain 12-semitone octave; stretch or compress the partials and it drifts off 12 — your target for highest consonance follows the timbre.
