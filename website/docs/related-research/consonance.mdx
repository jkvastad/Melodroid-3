# Consonance
When two or more frequencies sound simultaneously they are perceived as more or less pleasant. This is called consonance.
The reasons for consonance have perhaps been studied for millenia - already the babylonians had tuning systems.[^west]

In modern times there are three primary hypotheses for consonance.[^mcbride]
* Aversion to roughness
* Preference for harmonicity
* Learned preference from cultural exposure

Aversion to roughness centers around Helmholtz observation[^helmholtz] that as two coinciding frequencies are separated, there occurs a maxima of unpleasantness related to frequency beating. 
This creates a "roughness" or "dissonance" curve.

Preference for harmonicity centers around the idea that humans like it when interval and overtone ratios are small simple fractions e.g. the octaves 2:1 or the perfect fifths 3:2.

Learned preference centers around the idea that musical preference is a learned phenomena.

## Timbral & Harmonic Effects
The pleasantness of simultaneous chord is affected not only by frequency ratios, but also by timbre (overtone/partials profiles).
In ["Timbral effects on consonance disentangle psychoacoustic mechanisms and suggest perceptual origins for musical scales"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11258268/)[^marjieh],
the authors run a series of experiments showing that consonance judgements are affected by not just the intervals (harmonicity) but by overtone profiles as well (aversion to roughness). 
These experiments are reimplemented below for the reader to experience for themselves:

Both notes share the same overtone profile: with harmonic partials ($\gamma = 2.0$) the plain octave (12 semitones) locks into consonance. 
As the partials are stretched or compressed the consonant interval drifts away from 12 semitones — the octave is consonant only relative to its timbre.

The red dashed line marks $12\log_2\gamma$ above the lower note - the semitone where all partials lock. 
This is where the upper note's frequency ratio equals the stretch $\gamma$, so every upper partial lands on a lower partial and the whole spectrum locks.

In fact, for any step size $s$ with interval ratio $r = 2^{s/12}$, consider an interval of two tones whose partials are powers of that ratio i.e. $\{1, r, r^2, \dots\}$.
Since $r$ is the ratio of these two tones of frequency $a$ and $b$, then if $b > a$ it follows that

$$
ar = b \iff r = b/a
$$

This implies that for any overtones $a_i$ and $b_j$ we have

$$
a_i = a r^i = a (b/a)^i = b^i / a^{i-1}
$$

$$
b_j = b r^j = b (b/a)^j = b^{j+1} / a^{j}
$$

with equality when $j + 1 = i$.

This means every partial of the upper note lands on a partial of the lower note. Try this out in the graph below. The first slider blends from ordinary
harmonic partials toward this matched spectrum, and the second sets the step size. 
The third slider sets how many partials sound (1–12): adding more does not make the dyad more dissonant, as it has been reported[^mclachlan] that adding more harmonics does not increase dissonance perception.
This implies that dissonance perception is not just a result of beating partials.

The study finds consonance peaks at $\approx 2.6$ and $\approx 4.8$ semitones. This is where the bonang's $3.46\,f_0$ and $1.52\,f_0$ partials meet the lower tone's 4th and 2nd harmonics.
These sit on the marked Slendro degrees (an equal five-tone division of the octave, ~240 cents per step), which the authors read as a perceptual origin for the scale.


## Effects from Culture and Experience
Consonance perception is not only affected by rougness and harmonicity as can be perceived in the examples above, but also by individual and cultural experience.

### Across Experience
People report dissonance differently depending on how exactly it is queried in relation to their musical expertise, 
with e.g. pleasantness varying greatly but tension being invariant.[^lahdelma]

When asked about dissonance as a form of "perceived roughness, harshness, unpleasantness, or difficulty in listening to the sound" 
people report lower dissonance for the same dyads after having practiced pitch-matching (calibrating a tone until it is perceived to match a tone in the dyad).[^mclachlan] 
From these results it is proposed that dissonance is affected by cognitive incongruence when expectations clash with perceived reality.

Octave equivalence is generally hard to hear but can be improved somewhat through training.[^schmidt]

### Across Culture
A series of experiments involving the Tsimane people from Bolivia have produced interesting findings.
The Tsimane have a music tradition consisting of performances from one singer/musician (flute, violin) at a time and rarely with accompanying instruments, producing little experience with harmony.

The Tsimane have an aversion to roughness/tension, but do not consider chords such as major thirds more pleasant than e.g. a tritone or minor second.[^mcdermott]

The Tsimane do experience many important aspects of music similarly to westerners (US participants, i.e. 12tet culture) when probed with sung reproduction of tone sequences, with some differences:[^jacoby]
* Mental scaling of pitch was approximately logarithmic for US and Amazonian listeners (e.g. when reproducing a heard interval at another $f_0$ the interval ratio is kept)
* Pitch perception deteriorated similarly in both groups for very high-frequency tones (4khz+ $f_0$)
* Sung correlates of octave equivalence varies across cultures and musical expertise
  * When reproducing a heard interval in their singing range, US participants (especially trained musicians) would try to match their replicated pitches so as to be octave equivalent with the heard pitches.
  * The Tsimane did not - even when instructed to actively try to do so.
A possibility of note is that the lack of universal octave equivalence might be due to a lack of universal absolute pitch perception.

These findings of culture and experience point to some features being innate to humans: 
* Sensitivity to harmonicity (we notice integer ratio overtones)
* Perceptual fusion of simple-ratio intervals[^mcpherson] (in fact we easily perceive integer ratio overtones as a whole)
* Logarithmic pitch scaling (we hear ratios)
* and the upper limit on melodic pitch (4khz)

Two things often assumed to be universal turn out to be substantially cultural: 
* Pleasure of consonance (everyone notices harmonicity but not everyone values it aesthetically)
* Octave-equivalence (not everyone perceives doubled frequencies as the same pitch class)

## Consequences for Harmonic Pattern Theory
What does all this mean for harmonic pattern theory?

When listening to intervals (good fractions) and sets of frequencies (lcm families) we should expect consonance to depend on all three major contributors disscused above: roughness, culture and harmonics.

We should also expect there to be general universal features such as fusion, upper limits of melodic pitch, logarithmic pitch scaling and sensitivity to harmonicity. 
Some aspects related to consonance seem to be universal (tension) but the aesthetics (pleasing/displeasing) is cultural.
Perceiving pitch classes across octaves seems to be cultural as well, but for our purposes of folding fractions onto the interval [1,2) for clean math the cross-cultural tonal fusion might be a sufficient rationale.[^jacoby]
