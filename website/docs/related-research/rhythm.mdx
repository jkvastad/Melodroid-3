# Rhythm
Sound waves are in the abstract amplitude modulations,
with the frequency of modulation being perceived as pitch and the amplitude as loudness.
This loudness can change over time and thus carry patterns for the brain to interpret.

The loudness pulses are often called beats, especially when isochronous, with different structures of pulses having different names.
The most common groupings are meter and rhythm. 
* Meter is a more strict organization of beats into recurring groups of strong and weak pulses (*One*-two-three, *One*-two-three...),
* Rhythm is the pattern of sounds and silences as played.
By dividing a meter into smaller (possibly silent) pulses one can create rhythms by sounding these subdivisions.

## Additive and Divisive Patterns
Meter (and rhythm) can be categorised as [additive or divisive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_rhythm_and_divisive_rhythm).

A **divisive** meter takes one span and splits it into equal units. 
The common 4/4 divides a measure into halves then quarters, e.g. strong, weak, medium, weak.

<SequencePlayer beatSec={0.2} gain={1} label="▶ 4/4 — divisive (strong-weak-medium-weak)" melody={pulses(['S', 'w', 'm', 'w'])} />

An **additive** meter instead concatenates groups of pulses which do not produce a divise rhythm e.g. $\frac{2}{8} + \frac{3}{8} = \frac{5}{8}$. 
This necessitates uneven groupings which produces a characteristic limping feel well known in [aksak music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aksak).
Note that the pulse rate is identical to the 4/4 example above — only the grouping changes, but the feel is drastically different.

## Rhythmic Perference of Integer Ratios
When presented with random sequences of three interval rhythms (e.g. $1.6:2.14:1$) and asked to iteratively reproduce the rhythms, 
both western (US) and non-western (Bolivian Tsimane) listeners converge to simple integer ratios over a handful of iterations[^jacoby] 
(notably this convergence effect translates poorly to speech). 

This was later reproduced in 15 countries on 5 continents[^jacoby2] - everyone prefers integer ratios, but the exact ratios preferred vary by culture.
For example in Mali there is a relatively popular Maraka rhythm of $7:2:3$

<SequencePlayer beatSec={0.2} gain={1} label="▶ 12/8 Maraka — 7+2+3 ([2+2+3]+2+3)" melody={pulses(['S', 'w', 'm', 'w', 'm', 'm', 'w', 'm', 'w', 'm', 'w', 'w'])} />

and also Manjanin/Woloso[^polak] with $3:4:5$ (slowed down to show the structure)
<SequencePlayer beatSec={0.2} gain={1} label="▶ 12/8 Manjanin/Woloso — 3+4+5" melody={pulses(['S', 'w', 'w', 'm', 'w', 'w', 'w', 'm', 'w', 'w', 'w', 'w'])} />

<SequencePlayer beatSec={0.2} gain={1} label="▶ 12/8 Manjanin/Woloso — 3+4+5 (3+[2+2+2]+3)" melody={pulses(['S', 'w', 'w', 'm', 'w', 'm', 'w', 'm', 'w', 'm', 'w', 'w'])} />

Original recordings can be found at [Polak's field recordings site](https://www.rainerpolak.de/field-recordings/audio-albums/).

A consequence of integer ratio rhythms is that there exists a (possibly silent/virtual) reference pulse since all integer ratios are by definition multiples of the integer one.
Such a fastest pulse is also known as the [Tatum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tatum_(music)). 

An isochronous pulse train consisting of the fastest pulse can be grouped by twos and threes to produce any ratios e.g. $7+2+3 = (2+2+3)+2+3$.
The groupings themselves are not isochronous as they do have different sizes ($7 \neq 2 \neq 3$), and in general isochrony is not needed for rhythm, 
nor is is important that the fastest pulse has a fixed time length over a piece as it may accelerate/decelarate.[^polak]

## Perceiving and Producing Polyrythms
Above we listened to examples of additive and divise meters and rhythms. Now we turn to simultaneous rhythms. Perhaps the simplest polyrythm is $3:2$:

<SequencePlayer beatSec={0.2} gain={1} label="▶ 3:2 polyrhythm" melody={pulses(['m', 's', 'm', 'm', 'm', 's'])} />

If you try to tap this rhythm using first one hand for the $2:1$ beat and then adding in the other for the $3:1$ you will most likely run into problems.
If instead you try to structure it in your mind as one single pattern over six pulses it will suddenly become easy.[^maslovat] For example try thinking of it as two blocks:
* both hands (2 pulses)
* left (1), right(1), left(2)

<SequencePlayer beatSec={0.2} gain={1} label="▶ 3:2 polyrhythm" melody={polyrhythm(2, 3)} />

With 10 pulses we have $5:2$
<SequencePlayer beatSec={0.2} gain={1} label="▶ 2:5 polyrhythm" melody={polyrhythm(2,5)} />
* both hands(2 pulses), left(2)
* left(1), right(1)
* left(2), left(2)

With 12 pulses we have $4:3$
<SequencePlayer beatSec={0.2} gain={1} label="▶ 4:3 polyrhythm" melody={polyrhythm(4,3)} />
* both hands(3 pulses)
* right(1), left(2), right(2)
* left(1), right(3)

The above examples are played at the same tempo - the fastest pulse has the same length for all examples. 
If you listen to them and tap along you will find a beat division of the pattern. Changing the tempo changes the preference for where this beat is perceived.[^moller]

## Tempo and the Perceived Beat
The same polyrhythm affords more than one metrical reading. A $2:3$ polyrhythm can be heard either as a
**2-beat** pulse train — each beat split into three tatums (a *ternary* subdivision) — or as a **3-beat**
pulse train — each beat split into two tatums (a *binary* subdivision). Møller and colleagues asked which
of these listeners spontaneously tap to, and how that depends on tempo.[^moller]

Their finding is that listeners overwhelmingly favour the stream that admits a **binary** subdivision (here
the 3-beat stream). This preference holds across slow and moderate tempi *regardless of tempo*; only when the
pulse gets very fast do listeners jump up to the whole-cycle level rather than ever adopting the ternary
2-beat stream. The preferred beat itself sits around $80$–$160$ BPM. In short: time is structured in binary units.

Start a looping player below, tap along until a beat settles, then drag the tempo slider. Notice that as you
sweep from slow to fast the felt beat tends to stay on the binary-subdividable stream — the slider caption
shows each stream's beat rate in BPM so you can see which candidate you have locked onto.
