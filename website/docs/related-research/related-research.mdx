# Related Research
The idea of pattern matching to wave patterns has several parallels in published literature.

## Wave Pattern Length
Several works have discussed the length of wave patterns in one form or another:

### Relative Periodicity
In Harmony Perception by Periodicity Detection[^stolzenburg] Frieder Stolzenburg discusses **relative periodicity** as 
"the approximated ratio of the period length of the chord (its periodicity pitch) relative to the period length of its lowest tone component 
(without necessarily applying octave equivalence)". This is our WPL, but Stolzenburg presents it in a more rigorous context. For approximating ratios the 1% JND is used.

### Gradus Suavitatis
Euler discussed the **gradus suavitatis**[^euler] (degree of softness) as a measure of harmoniousness.
As explained by Stolzenburg[^stolzenburg], it considers the least common multiple (LCM) of the
denominators and numerators of the frequency ratios $\{a_1/b_1, \dots, a_k/b_k\}$ (where all fractions
$a_i/b_i$ are in lowest terms) with respect to the lowest tone in the given harmony. If
$p_1^{m_1} \cdots p_\ell^{m_\ell}$ is the prime factorisation of

$$
n = \operatorname{lcm}(a_1, \dots, a_k) \cdot \operatorname{lcm}(b_1, \dots, b_k),
$$

then the gradus suavitatis is defined by

$$
\Gamma(n) = 1 + \sum_{i=1}^{\ell} m_i \cdot (p_i - 1).
$$

For instance, for the major second interval consisting of the frequency ratios $\{1/1, 9/8\}$ (including
the root), we have $\operatorname{lcm}(1, 9) \cdot \operatorname{lcm}(1, 8) = 72 = 2^3 \cdot 3^2$ and hence
$\Gamma(72) = 1 + 3 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 2 = 8$ as the value of the gradus suavitatis.

This is similar to our observations regarding LCM for denominators and WPL, 
and how renormalizing a reference point means that numerators may also play a role in deciding WPL for a set of fractions.

## Good Fractions
### Rational Tunings
When discussing relative periodicity, Stolzenburg[^stolzenburg] assumes 12 TET and then searches for fractions which are close to the 12 keys by increasing the error margin.
This leads to two sets, his **rational tuning #1** (max deviation $d = 1.0\%$) and **rational tuning #2** ($d = 1.1\%$). 
Both are built by taking, for each of the 12 keys, the fraction with the smallest denominator lying within the tolerance band of 12-TET.
This is the same "smallest fraction within the JND" idea behind the good fractions, but mapping fractions onto keys rather than keys onto fractions.

| Semitone | Interval | Rational tuning #1 | Rational tuning #2 | A good fraction? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 0 | Unison | 1/1 | 1/1 | ✓ |
| 1 | Minor second | 16/15 | 16/15 | ✓ |
| 2 | Major second | 9/8 | 9/8 | ✓ (10/9 also good, unused) |
| 3 | Minor third | 6/5 | 6/5 | ✓ |
| 4 | Major third | 5/4 | 5/4 | ✓ |
| 5 | Perfect fourth | 4/3 | 4/3 | ✓ |
| 6 | Tritone | 17/12 | 7/5 | ✗ — no good fraction |
| 7 | Perfect fifth | 3/2 | 3/2 | ✓ |
| 8 | Minor sixth | 8/5 | 8/5 | ✓ |
| 9 | Major sixth | 5/3 | 5/3 | ✓ |
| 10 | Minor seventh | 16/9 | 9/5 | ✓ (both are good) |
| 11 | Major seventh | 15/8 | 15/8 | ✓ |
| 12 | Octave | 2/1 | 2/1 | ✓ (≡ 1/1) |

The tunings diverge only at the **tritone** (17/12 vs 7/5) and the **minor seventh** (16/9 vs 9/5).

The comparison to the good fractions is striking: every ratio in either tuning is one of the standard 13 good fractions — **except the tritone**.
The tritone requires reaching outside the prime limit of 5, using either 7 (in 7/5) or 17 (in 17/12).

Two further differences:

- Standard good fractions contains **10/9** as an alternate major second, which neither tuning uses (both prefer 9/8).
- Standard good fractions contains **both** minor-seventh candidates 16/9 and 9/5, whereas each tuning commits to just one.

The good fractions effectively span the union of rational tunings #1 and #2, minus the tritone. 
Of note is that we use an additional constraint, that of max WPL, to constrain fraction size and limit primes. This then defines a set of good fractions.
From this we can reason about how many notes a tuning should have to express all good fractions, rather than assume 12 and see which fractions can express the notes.
