Several of these are harmonic-sounding chords and scales (Major 13 is the major scale). LCM 15 and 20 map to no traditional chords and sound a bit tense.
Traditionally consonant voicings favor triads (intervals 3, 4 in 12-TET) and avoid semitones (interval 1) - 
both easier on the hand and lower in dissonance.[^lots-stone] 
Consonance however is a complex subject, depending both on harmonicity, timbre and culture - 
[see related research on consonance.](/docs/related-research/consonance)

## Scoring voicings

To create some nicely spread out voicings we can rank a voicing by scoring each interval and summing:

- intervals in $\{3, 4\}$ (triadic) cost $0$;
- intervals in $\{2, 5\}$ (near-triadic) cost $1$;
- intervals $i \geq 6$ (wide) cost $i - 4$ (so $6 \to 2$, $7 \to 3$, …, $k{-}1 \to k{-}5$).

A pure-triadic voicing scores $0$; the score grows as the voicing is forced into nears and then
wides.

```bash
melodroid table voicings --lcm 24 --ktet 12
```

prints the lowest-penalty voicings of the C major scale (`24@0`) from every root. 
Pass `--keys` instead of `--lcm` to enumerate voicings for an arbitrary key set, e.g. `--keys 0 2 3 5 7 8 11` for C harmonic minor scale in 12 tet.

## High LCMs

As can be heard in the intro table, lcms of 12 or less have a softer sound while the larger lcms of 15 or higher have more tension, especially 15 and 20.
The best voicings for the high lcms are:

* `15@0` with `0 3 7 11 14 17 22` at score 1 
* `18@0` with `0 3 7 10 14 17` at score 0
* `20@0` with `0 4 8 11 15 18` at score 0
* `24@0` with `0 4 7 10 14 17 21` at score 0

Even the best voicings for 15 and 20 sound tense. 
Perhaps it is a failing of HPT, or perhaps they _should_ sound tense e.g. the "flavour" of lcm 15 might be tense and 20 is a subset via renormalisation (20@5 $\subset$ 15@0).
Lcm 15 and 20 might for instance be useful scales rather than block chords, or the tension is perhaps useful in and of itself (e.g. uniform chords dim7, aug).
Other reasons for sounding tense include:
* Increasingly large clusters of notes become increasingly harder to keep consonant.
* The largest lcm family recognized by the brain might be the isomorphic lcm 8-12 group. Higher lcms are perhaps parsed as superpositions of lower lcms.
* Some lcms might be preferred over others, e.g. 15 is close to 24 in the sense that dropping key 9 from 15@0 gives 
`{0 1 3 5 8 10}` which full-matches 18 at key 3 and 24 at both 1 and 8. 
Lcm 15 might then be parsed as 24 with bad notes, and perhaps only under certain circumstances such as including not 8 (root for 24@8, 3/2 ratio relative 24@1).

### High LCMs as compositions of low LCMs
A high lcm family can sometimes be expressed as a composition of lower lcm families.
Since all families $\leq$ 12 are subsets or isomorphisms of each other it seems prudent to use this largest isomorphic group (lcm 8-12) as the building block.
This can be explored using the superpositions subcommand:

`melodroid table superpositions --keys 0 2 4 5 7 9 11 --min-block-lcm 8 --max-block-lcm 12`

#### LCM 24
Running the superpositions command produces the (truncated) output below for 24@0 = `{0 2 4 5 7 9 11}`

| # | Ref | LCM | Pieces | Union (12-tet) | Extra | Disjoint? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 2 | 0 | 24 | 8@0 + 12@0 | 0 2 4 5 7 9 11 | | |
| 2 | 7 | 36 | 9@7 + 12@7 | 0 2 4 5 7 9 11 | | |

From this we see that 24@0 can (at least) be thought of as 8@0 + 12@0 or 9@7 + 12@7. 
These two pairs of small lcms have overlapping sets: 

8@0 = `{0 2 4 7 11}`, 12@0 = `{0 4 5 7 9}` $\rightarrow$ 8@0 $\cap$ 12@0 = `{0 4 7}`

9@7 = `{7 9 0 4 5}`, 12@7 = `{7 11 0 2 4}` $\rightarrow$ 9@7 $\cap$ 12@7 = `{0 4 7}`

Which turn out to be the same the major chord (which is just 4@0 $\subset$ 8@0), 
suggesting that perhaps there is no lcm 24 but instead it is better thought of as these superpositions.

#### LCM 20
For 20@0 = `{0 3 4 5 7 8 10}` we have

| # | Ref | LCM | Pieces | Union (12-tet) | Extra | Disjoint? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 2 | 0 | 60 | 10@0 + 12@0 | 0 3 4 5 7 8 9 10 | 9 | |

The extra 9 means there is no perfect composition of smaller lcms, perhaps why a full voicing of 20 sounds tense.
Looking at overlapp we see

10@0 = `{0 3 7 8 10}`, 12@0 = `{0 4 5 7 9}` $\rightarrow$ 10@0 $\cap$ 12@0 = `{0 7}`

`{0 7}` being the smallest good fraction has quite a lot of possible interpretations. 
If lcms above 12 are compositions of lower lcms then the keys corresponding to a hypothetical lcm 20 might not simply produce anything of particular interest,
 explaining the tense sound of lcm 20 (random noise).

#### LCM 18
For 18@0 = `{0 2 5 7 9 10}` we have

| # | Ref | LCM | Pieces | Union (12-tet) | Extra | Disjoint? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 2 | 0 | 36 | 9@0 + 12@0 | 0 2 4 5 7 9 10 | 4 | |
| 2 | 5 | 24 | 8@5 + 12@5 | 0 2 4 5 7 9 10 | 4 | |
| 2 | 5 | 36 | 9@5 + 12@5 | 0 2 3 5 7 9 10 | 3 | |
| 2 | 10 | 24 | 8@10 + 12@10 | 0 2 3 5 7 9 10 | 3 | |

Every union carries an extra note (4 or 3), meaning there is no perfect composition of smaller lcms.

The overlaps come in two pairs:

9@0 = `{0 2 5 9 10}`, 12@0 = `{0 4 5 7 9}` $\rightarrow$ 9@0 $\cap$ 12@0 = `{0 5 9}`

8@5 = `{5 7 9 0 4}`, 12@5 = `{5 9 10 0 2}` $\rightarrow$ 8@5 $\cap$ 12@5 = `{0 5 9}`

9@5 = `{5 7 10 2 3}`, 12@5 = `{5 9 10 0 2}` $\rightarrow$ 9@5 $\cap$ 12@5 = `{2 5 10}`

8@10 = `{10 0 2 5 9}`, 12@10 = `{10 2 3 5 7}` $\rightarrow$ 8@10 $\cap$ 12@10 = `{2 5 10}`

Notably lcm 18 and 24 share the same structure: lcm 9 + 12 or 8 + 12 share a root, so perhaps lcm 18 is also better thought of as a superposition of lower lcms.

#### LCM 15
For 15@0 = `{0 1 3 5 8 9 10}` we have

| # | Ref | LCM | Pieces | Union (12-tet) | Extra | Disjoint? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 2 | 5 | 60 | 10@5 + 12@5 | 0 1 2 3 5 8 9 10 | 2 | |

There is only one composition of two smaller lcms, which also has the best fit but still requiring an extra note (key 2).
The overlap is

10@5 = `{5 8 0 1 3}`, 12@5 = `{5 9 10 0 2}` $\rightarrow$ 10@5 $\cap$ 12@5 = `{0 5}`

`{0 5}` is a bare fifth (2@5), again the smallest good fraction — as with lcm 20's `{0 7}` the two pieces barely overlap, perhaps why a full voicing of 15 also sounds tense.

#### Concluding remarks
Looking at high LCMs as compositions of low lcms we have the following patterns:

* 24@0 is (perfectly) 
  * 8@0 + 12@0 
  * 9@7 + 12@7
* 20@0 is 10@0 + 12@0 (extra 9)
* 18@0 is 
  * 9@0 + 12@0 (extra 4)
  * 8@5 + 12@5 (extra 4)  
  * 9@5 + 12@5 (extra 3)
  * 8@10 + 12@10 (extra 3)
* 15@0 is 10@5 + 12@5 (extra 2)

From this we see that the structure of 18@0 (adding in the extra key) is identical to 24@5 and 24@10.
Similarly lcm 15 and 20 share structure with 15@0 being 20@5 expressed as 10@5 + 12@5.
These pairs of structures are not so different, yet 15 and 20 sound quite more tense than 24/18. 
Perhaps it is that the total lcm of the composing smaller lcms is much greater for 15/20 (lcm 60) vs. 18/24 with 24 (or 36).

### Adjacent key sets and preference
Assuming the brain does not passively parse incoming sounds but rather looks actively for patterns, we would expect preference for certain patterns.
This could for instance mean that some lcms are preferred over others. 
Since most lcms are subsets of each other certain contexts might cause an "obvious" pattern to be ignored in favor of a preferred pattern.
By looking at subsets of lcms we can gain some insight into these possible competing interpretations.
For this purpose we have the subsets subcommand:

```bash
melodroid table subsets --lcm 15 --at 0 --ktet 12
```

## LCM melodies
To play melodies we will need to move from playing block chords in static time into dynamic time. 
This produces structures of beats implicit in the change of notes (see [Rhythm chapter](/docs/related-research/rhythm) for related research and basic concepts).

### Rhythmic Patterns
Rhythmic patterns can be constructed by:

* Selecting a meter consisting of a series of integers e.g. `{4}` or `{7,2,3}`. These number signify the heavy accents on beats in the reccuring pattern.
* Selecting a tempo (beats per minute, bpm) by choosing the rate of the (possibly implicit) unit beat. 
Since meters come in integer ratios there is always a unit beat e.g. `{4}={4,1}`.
* Selecting a grid resolution or fastest pulse by choosing the largest division of the unit beat. 
This subdivision is typically in the range of 1-8 since maximum groove is about 100-120bpm, producing a fastest pulse of 62.5ms. 
Any faster and the pulse starts intruding on the pitch domain.
* Accenting pulses (possibly silent) in the pattern. This decides long, heavy, light, short, moderate, etc., note amplitude and length.
When accenting pulses a moderate deviation from the meter produces the highest groove.

The above procedure produces an amplitude pattern consisting of meter and rhythm. 
This pattern can then be decorated with pitch at active (non-silent) pulses.

Try it below. Enter a **meter** (one number per accent
group, e.g. `4` or `7 2 3`) and a **subdivision** (a single number for the whole meter,
or one per group), then shape the pattern with two sliders:

* **Resolution** — how much of the pulse grid is active. At the low end only the main
beats fire; at the high end every subdivision does.
* **Syncopation** — deviation from the meter. At zero the heavy accents sit exactly on
the meter beats; as it rises the accents redistribute, drifting onto off-beat pulses
while some meter beats drop to lighter accents.

Press **Generate** to generate a concrete pattern from the current settings, and **Play** to loop it. The plot shows each pulse's velocity
against its position in unit beats, with grid lines for the meter accents (bold), unit
beats (medium), and the full pulse grid (faint). **Tempo** adjusts live while playing.

### Melody
A naive approach to lcm melody is to randomly select pitch values from an lcm family to decorate a rhythmic pattern.

The player below adds an lcm dropdown listing the LCM families from the table at the top of the page. 
Pulses are pitched with a note drawn at random from the selected family's notes, folded into a single octave.
Each bar is tinted along the visible spectrum by the pitch it carries — low
pitch (long wavelength) red, high pitch blue/violet. 

**Loop melody** bakes the drawn pitches into a repeating phrase; turn it off to re-roll a fresh pitch on every hit.
 **Generate** samples a new rhythm and a new melody to go with it.

 You can also listen to a chromatic scale, true random pitch, two familiar tonal scales (major pentatonic and harmonic minor), and the symmetric chords that divide the octave evenly (whole tone, diminished 7th, augmented) for comparison.
 The **instrument** dropdown switches both the melody and the chord between the theory-faithful sines and a sampled piano.[^salamander]

<RhythmPatternPlayer meter="4" subdivisions="2" bpm={100} melody />

Anecdotally the random pitches and chromatic scale sound quite similar, a quality perhaps best described as chaotic, psychadelic or confusing.
This quality is shared to some degree with the symmetric chords and harmonic minor. 

Lcm families up to and including 12 instead sound pleasant/soft/round/free of tension, a quality shared with the major pentatonic scale (subset of 24@0 among others). 
Lcms 15 and above produce more tension, or perhaps randomness. 
This is especially true for lcm 15 and 20 with sound reminiscent of the random/chromatic pitches.

Notably the default tones used here are sines and do not sound wrong in and of themselves (no overtones to produce roughness).
Playing piano notes instead produces much the same sensatinos as sines, notably the truly random pitches sound even more pronounced with a metalic quality.

Where does the chaotic sound come from? There are at least two flavors - random/tense and harmonious/soft. 
Perhaps it comes from context - the listener expects certain pitches from preceding notes (or even having heard/recognizing a melody beforehand).
A common context in music is chords - a set of notes played statically along with the melody.

### Chords as accompaniment
Let's now look at chords. In their most general form chords are two or more simultaneous notes.
In the 12 tet system there are $2^{12} = 4096$ possible key combinations per octave, including the empty set.
In particular we are interested in unique chords under octave renormalization e.g. 0 4 7, 0 3 8 and 0 5 9 are the same.

Counting a chord and all its transpositions once collapses the 4096 combinations to just 352 unique chords (the [binary necklaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necklace_(combinatorics)) of length 12), or 280 across the 2–7 note range we care about.
These can be enumerated with the `chords` subcommand:

```bash
melodroid table chords --min-notes 2 --max-notes 7
```

Additionally most chords do not sound particularly good with minor second or tritone. 
Removing minor seconds produces 29 chords.
Removing tritones produces 61 chords.
Removing both produces 16 chords.

There are however some chords which do have a minor second (m2) of sorts - a major seventh (M7) in e.g. 0 4 7 11 14 (lcm 8@0) sounds quite nice, even if the interval 11 does not.
Interestingly, playing first 0 4 7 11 followed by 0 11, the 0 11 keeps the rounder/softer quality of the preceding chord. 
Follow this up with 1 12 to produce a harsh m2/M7 which then becomes soft by adding in the related 8@1 i.e. 1 5 8 12. 
This process can be repeated with e.g. 1 13 and so on.

Such an effect hints at that it matters not just what we hear, but what we recently heard. 
How recent is recent? Perhaps it is simply what we expect to hear, heavily influenced by what we just heard, but also by other expectations such as familiar melodies and songs.

In the context of accompaniment for a given chord we must then choose a set of notes from which to draw melody.

This presents us with two questions - how do we choose chords and how do we choose the corresponding melody set?
For constructing chords in we have several options:

* Random notes
* A subset to an lcm
* Symmetric chords, possibly a subset of larger symmetric structures (e.g. the whole tone scale)

In HPT the only governing structure is the lcms of the constituent good fractions. 
Choosing random chords should thus either produce lcm subsets by luck or no structure at all.
Symmetric chords are notable because of their ambiguity: it is not the lcm structure in and of itself which is ultimately interesting but our perception of the structure.

For constructing a melody set we also have several options:

* Random notes
* The notes constituting the chord
* Notes from an lcm for which the chord is a subset (full matches)

We previousy listened to random notes and observed they have a specific almost chaotic quality.
Melodic notes constituting a chord will likely inherit its qualities from whatever structure the chord has.
Notes from an lcm should similarly produce soft/harmonious melodies from lcms $\leq$ 12.

Notably a chord can be a subset to several lcms simultaneously and a union or intersection of such shared lcms might be an interesting melodic set,
providing some form of modulation between lcms.

The largest lcm family consist of 7 members, which is also large enough to include the whole tone scale and the harmonic minor.
We can thus create chords by selecting between 2 and 7 notes.
