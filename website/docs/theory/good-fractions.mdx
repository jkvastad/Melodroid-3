# Good Fractions

When expressing simultaneous notes as fractions of a reference note, some fractions are better
than others at forming a repeating pattern over a short time period. 

A **good fraction** is one whose numerator and denominator are both small and built only from small primes — the two
properties that keep the [wave pattern length](../intro.mdx#harmony-as-pattern-recognition-of-simultaneous-waves) short.
The idea is that the brain hears real valued frequencies and maps them onto good fractions for pattern matching.

The reason numerators also contribute to low WPL is because music is octave-equivalent, meaning all fractions are mapped onto $[1, 2)$. 
Any note in a set may be renormalised as the base wavelength; resulting fractions below one are doubled until they land in $[1, 2)$. 

For instance, $\{1,\ 5/4,\ 3/2\}$ renormalised to base $5/4$ becomes $\{4/5,\ 1,\ 6/5\}$, which octave-normalises to $\{1,\ 6/5,\ 8/5\}$.
The numerator then becomes a factor in the denominator, illustrating why numerators share the same constraints as denominators.

## We Happy Few

Fractions decide the length of a wave pattern, but recall that wave pattern lengths have an upper bound via a wave pattern duration of some $50\ \mathrm{ms}$. At $100\ \mathrm{Hz}$ a single wave takes $10\ \mathrm{ms}$ and would
cap the WPL at $5$, allowing very few patterns. However, this limit is an estimate, and the real ceiling could be higher. Which fractions then are useful for constructing patterns?

Two factors keep the WPL manageable:

- **Small numerators/denominators.** The WPL is the $\operatorname{lcm}$ of the denominators,
  so small values keep it low; by octave equivalence the numerators inherit the same
  constraint.
- **Small primes.** Because the WPL is an $\operatorname{lcm}$, denominators built from small primes ($2, 3, 5$)
  cancel against each other when a set is renormalised to a different base, keeping the total
  WPL lenient.

In practice a WPL of up to **24** is of interest. This is heavily influenced by the fact that the major
scale played as a chord (the $G13 / \mathrm{Cmaj}13$ voicing) is a pattern of WPL $24$. WPL $24$ also allows the 12-tone equal temperament (12-TET) semitone approximation $16/15$, as well as a combination of WPL $8$ and $3$.

## Definition

The possible good fractions are therefore a function of two parameters:

- `--max-size` — the largest allowed numerator or denominator (default $24$);
- `--max-prime` — the largest prime allowed in their factorisations (default $5$).

Every good fraction is mapped onto $[1, 2)$ by octave equivalence.

## Listing Fractions

```bash
melodroid table good-fractions
```

Options: `--max-size` (default 24), `--max-prime` (default 5). See the
[CLI reference](../cli/reference.mdx#good-fractions) for the full command surface.

With the defaults (`--max-size 24`, `--max-prime 5`) this yields 13 good fractions:

$$
\frac{1}{1},\ \frac{16}{15},\ \frac{10}{9},\ \frac{9}{8},\ \frac{6}{5},\ \frac{5}{4},\ \frac{4}{3},\ \frac{3}{2},\ \frac{8}{5},\ \frac{5}{3},\ \frac{16}{9},\ \frac{9}{5},\ \frac{15}{8}
$$
